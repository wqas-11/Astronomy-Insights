Comets

Introduction

Comets are icy celestial bodies that originate from the outer regions of the solar system. When they approach the Sun, they develop visible comas and tails due to the sublimation of their volatile components.

Structure and Composition

  • Nucleus: The solid core of a comet, composed of ice, dust, and rocky material.
  • Coma: A cloud of gas and dust that forms around the nucleus as it heats up.
  • Tails: Comets have two tails—an ion tail made of charged particles and a dust tail composed of small solid particles. These tails always point away from the Sun due to the solar wind and radiation pressure.

Notable Comets

  • Halley's Comet: One of the most famous periodic comets, visible from Earth every 75-76 years.
  • Comet Hale-Bopp: A bright comet that was visible to the naked eye for a record 18 months in the late 1990s.
  • Comet NEOWISE: Discovered in 2020, it provided a spectacular display visible from Earth.

Scientific Importance

Comets are considered time capsules of the early solar system, containing primitive material that dates back to its formation. Studying them provides valuable information about the origins of the solar system and the building blocks of life.
